02

Anatomy of a hole

A black hole is not an object so much as a set of nested boundaries in spacetime. From the outside in, each one marks a point of no return for something — first your stable orbit, then light itself, then everything.

  1. Accretion disk

    In-falling gas spirals tighter and faster, heating by friction to millions of kelvin and glowing across the spectrum. The brightest thing in the picture sits just outside the dark.

  2. Ergosphere rotating only

    Around a spinning hole, space itself is dragged along. Inside this region nothing can stay still — yet you can still escape, carrying away some of the hole's rotational energy.

  3. Innermost stable orbit · ISCO

    At 3 rs lies the last circular orbit matter can hold. Cross inward and there is no stable path; you spiral down. It sets the disk's bright inner edge.

  4. Photon sphere

    At 1.5 rs gravity bends light into a circle. Photons can orbit the hole — and a beam grazing this radius wraps around to form the bright ring you see lensed in the render.

  5. Event horizon

    The surface of no return, at the Schwarzschild radius rs = 2GM/c². To stay put here you'd need to travel at light speed. Cross it and every future points inward.

  6. Singularity

    At the center, the equations of general relativity predict infinite density in zero volume — a sign that the theory itself breaks down and quantum gravity must take over.

03

What gravity this strong does

Five consequences of bending spacetime to its limit — the second one you can run yourself.

i

Gravitational lensing

Mass curves the paths of light. A black hole curves them so sharply that you see the far side of its own accretion disk lifted above and below the hole — and distant stars smeared into Einstein rings. The render does this by integrating each ray's geodesic.

ii

Time dilation

Clocks run slower in deep gravity. At radius r, one second near the hole stretches to 1 / √(1 − rs/r) seconds for a distant observer. Slide inward toward the horizon and watch the two clocks drift apart.

Observer radius 3.00 rs

1 second here = 1.22 s far away.

iii

Spaghettification

Gravity pulls your feet harder than your head. Near a stellar-mass hole the difference across your body becomes billions of g — you're stretched into a thread. Curiously, the bigger the hole, the gentler the horizon: you'd cross a supermassive one intact.

iv

Hawking radiation

Quantum fields near the horizon let a black hole glow ever so faintly and slowly evaporate. A solar-mass hole is colder than deep space (≈ 6×10⁻⁸ K) and would take ~10⁶⁷ years to vanish — but the smaller it gets, the hotter and faster it burns.

v

The information paradox

If a hole evaporates completely, what happens to the information that fell in? Quantum mechanics says it can't be destroyed; naïve relativity says it's gone. Reconciling the two is one of the deepest open problems in physics.
